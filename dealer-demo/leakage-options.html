Price leakage — three ways to frame it

Same sample dealer, three designs. The current tool uses a 4th, weaker approach ("vs list price"), which over-states leakage at ₺1.77M. Compare and tell me which to build.

Option A · smallest change

Discount discipline — vs your own target, not vs sticker

Keep the table, but change the benchmark from list price to the dealer's own target discount. Only the over-discounting beyond policy counts as leakage — honest and recoverable. Notice used cars are inside target, so they leak nothing.

Reads honestly: ₺234k of recoverable over-discount — not ₺1.77M of fantasy "vs sticker." A dealer trusts this.

Pro: quick, credible. Con: still just vehicles; doesn't touch service/parts where real money hides.

Option B · most impressive to a finance buyer

Pocket-price waterfall — list → what you actually keep

Decompose how a new car's ₺95,000 list erodes to a ₺90,000 pocket price, and split the ₺5,000 gap into structural (OEM campaign support — reimbursed, not your fault) vs controllable (negotiated discount, trade over-allowance). Leakage = only the controllable part.

The insight: of the ₺5,000 gap per car, only ₺3,500 is controllable (₺700k across 200 cars). The ₺1,500 OEM support isn't a loss at all — most tools wrongly count it as leakage.

Pro: rigorous, separates real loss from noise — a CFO/pricing buyer loves it. Con: needs the dealer to know the campaign-support & trade-allowance split per deal.

Option C · most useful to an actual dealer

Where the money really leaks — by department, ranked

Leakage means different things in each part of the store. Measure each against the dealer's own target and rank by money. The big one is usually not car discounts — it's the service labour rate.

The punchline: addressable leakage (₺924k) is bigger than the whole year's net profit (₺660k) — and most of it is in fixed-ops, not the showroom. That reframes the conversation completely.

Pro: operationally real, ranked, actionable; dealers feel each line. Con: needs a few more inputs (posted/effective rate, attach %, target margin).

My lean: C as the tab (it's what a dealer feels), with B's structural-vs-controllable idea baked into the car line so we never over-claim. But it's your call — tell me which.
