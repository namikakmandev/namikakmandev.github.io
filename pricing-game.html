The Pricing Game

You are the new Pricing Manager of Atlas Coffee Co. Your product: a premium 1 kg coffee bag. Unit cost $6.00, shelf price $10.00. You share the market with BrewCo ($10.50, mainstream — and it fights back), Velvet Roast ($13.50, premium) — and rumor says a discounter is coming.

You have 12 months. Each month the market throws something at you, and you make two decisions: your price and one strategic move (how to respond to the situation — each option has real effects on demand, trust and cash, and earns points).

Market share is fought over by all four brands. Price too high and shoppers drift to BrewCo. Chase the discounter and you burn margin. Raise prices at the wrong moment and customers stop trusting you. At year-end you are scored against the autopilot — a manager who never touches the price and always picks the safe move.

How the simulation works

Each brand gets an attractiveness score from its brand strength minus a price-sensitivity penalty, and market share is split between the four brands with a logit (softmax) choice model — with a one-month lag, because customers don't all switch at once. A customer trust index (0–100) scales your demand: sharp hikes hurt it, hikes during a shortage hurt it badly, stable pricing rebuilds it. Your strategic moves change trust, brand strength, demand or cash directly. BrewCo reacts to being undercut; a recession makes everyone more price-sensitive. Profit = (price − unit cost) × volume − $120k fixed costs ± one-off campaign costs. The autopilot benchmark plays the identical year at a frozen $10.00 with the neutral move every month. The 🎯 coach judges your price on a 4-month view, so it never rewards unsustainable gouging.
