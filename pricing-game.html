The Pricing Game

You are the new Pricing Manager of Atlas Coffee Co. Your product: a premium 1 kg coffee bag. Unit cost $6.00, current shelf price $10.00, main competitor at $10.50.

You have 12 months. Each month the market throws something at you — cost spikes, competitor price cuts, demand booms — and you make exactly one decision: your price.

Price too high and you lose customers. Too low and you burn margin — and start price wars. Raise prices at the wrong moment and customers stop trusting you. At the end, your result is compared to the autopilot — a manager who never touches the price.

How the simulation works

Market share follows relative price through a logistic response curve — undercut the competitor and share drifts toward you, with a one-month lag (customers don't all switch at once). Total category demand has mild price elasticity, and a customer trust index (0–100) scales your demand: sharp price hikes hurt it, hikes during a shortage hurt it badly, and stable pricing slowly rebuilds it. The competitor reacts to you — undercut them hard and they cut back next month. Profit = (price − unit cost) × volume − $120k fixed costs. The autopilot benchmark plays the identical year at a frozen $10.00.
